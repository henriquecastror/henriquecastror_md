A messy book of notes / Meu bloco de notas sobre tudo

3 Literature

This version: 2020-11-15

3.1 List of papers - Classic

  • Akerlof (1970): Must read about asymmetry of information.
  • Fama and French (1997)
  • Fama and Jensen (1983a)
  • Fama and Jensen (1983b)
  • La Porta et al. (1998): Must read about Law and Finance.
  • Jensen and Meckling (1976): The origins of Agency conflicts and Corporate Governance.
  • Jensen (1986)
  • Markowitz (1952): The origins of portfolio.

3.2 List of papers - Corporate Finance

  • Almeida et al. (2011): Nice application of DiD in Finance.
  • Strebulaev and Yang (2013): Why some firms go zero-leveraged?

3.3 List of papers - Governance

  • Bertrand and Mullainathan (2003): The paper that suggests that managers like a “quite life”.
  • Black et al. (2017): About construct validity of “Governance”
  • Boyd, Gove, and Solarino (2017): A must read paper about methodological issues in CG research.
  • Chen, Dong, and Lin (2020): A nice paper about institutional investment and CSR performance: they have a sound RDD application.
  • Dickinson (2011): A way to identify a firm’s stage of life using cash flows.
  • Schiehll and Martins (2016): About the different associations of Governance and Investor Protection.

3.4 List of papers - Open Science

  • RAC’s editorials: I recommend all RAC’s Editorials since 2018 about Open Science, Open data and related topics (https://rac.anpad.org.br/index.php/rac)
  • Babin et al. (2020): Replication methods in academic research.
  • Beugelsdijk, Witteloostuijn, and Meyer (2020): The JIBS editorial about Open Science
  • Harvey (2017): discusses the publication of “significant” results. Read to understand what is the publication bias.
  • Harvey (2019): A discussion about replications.
  • Spiegel (2019): About replication and open data.
  • Mendes-Da-Silva and Cerqueira Leal (2021): What is Salami Science?
  • Raaij (2018): About Data Reuse

3.5 List of materials - Econometrics

  • Abadie, Diamond, and Hainmueller (2010): An example of Synthetic Control.
  • Angrist and Pischke (2008): Good to learn about causality methods.
  • Angrist and Pischke (2014): Easier to read than previous one. I use this one in classes.
  • Bascle (2008): Also easy to read review about endogeneity and methods.
  • Bertrand, Duflo, and Mullainathan (2004)
  • Brooks (2019): Book with nice examples and lots of codes.
  • Bowen, Frésard, and Taillard (2017): Important to see the development of causal methods.
  • Cappelleri and Trochim (2015): Introduction to RDD.
  • Gow, Larcker, and Reiss (2016): Good review about causal methods.
  • Gormley and Matsa (2014): This is here so I remember to read (didn’t read yet).
  • Jiang (2017): A discussion about instrumental variables.
  • Kahn and Whited (2018): Identification is not causality, and vice-versa.
  • Lindner, Puck, and Verbeke (2019): Some ideas aboout multicollinearity.
  • Murray (2006): Nice examples of instruments
  • Petersen (2009)
  • Reeb, Sakakibara, and Mahmood (2012): Easy to reed review about endogeneity in International Business.
  • Roodman (2009b): Important to estimate GMM in stata.
  • Roodman (2009a): Important to estimate GMM in Stata.
  • Schiozer, Mourad, and Martins (2020): A tutorial about Diff-in-Diff, with open data and open code.
  • Stuart (2010)
  • Wasserstein and Lazar (2016): About p-value and the importance of not using a single metric to assess the quality of a model.
  • Wooldridge (2019): The basic must-to-read book about econometrics.

3.6 List of books - Finance

  • Ehrhardt and Brigham (2012): ótimo livro para graduação e MBAs, mas não gosto da forma da tradução.
  • Berk and Demarzo (2013): Meu livro preferido para graduação e MBAs. No entanto, a versão boa é a inglês.
  • Bodie, Kane, and Marcus (2013):

3.7 List of papers - Science

  • Aguinis and Vaschetto (2011): Good reflection about Editor’s roles.
